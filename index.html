Opening Statement

I do not subscribe to the concept of a "false religion." In my view, such a notion does not exist. Every religion has an entity behind its practices, and if one adheres to the specific rules and rituals, it is possible to gain favor in the eyes of the deity associated with that religion. This deity may have the power to bring about positive changes in one's life. However, depending on their character, the opposite could happen.

When it comes to religion, I do not concern myself with the behavior or practices of its adherents while seeking a deity to follow. I firmly believe that no one in this world truly knows how to represent their god or practice correctly. Humanity, in my view, often leads lives that contradict their beliefs or what they profess to follow, revealing a pervasive self-interest and inconsistency.

For this reason, I do not judge the characteristics of any deity based on the actions of their followers. Instead, I advocate for spiritual independence above all else. I have a saying that I adapted from the famous proverb, ‘Show me your friends and I will show you your future.’ My version is, ‘Show me your life, and I will show you your god.’

Introduction

When people reject the idea of God, I find it particularly interesting because most are only familiar with the deity who condemns the world for its sins, as depicted in the Bible. Given the Bible's widespread influence, it is understandable why this is the case. However, I often wonder: what if there are other gods? What if these gods do not promise eternal life but instead offer us profound scientific knowledge about our universe? What if they do not demand anything from us except our consent to engage with them through rituals? In return, they could guide our morals, influence our thoughts, and transform our desire to worship into an obsession with the knowledge they provide, inspiring us to honor them through our works, such as naming our projects and discoveries after them.

In this discussion, I will explore the plausibility of these ideas, identify who these other gods might be, and delve into their origins. I will also evaluate whether these deities can be trusted. Additionally, I will draw parallels between religious and non-religious individuals, highlighting how both groups fulfill the common human desire to worship. I believe that humans are inherently inclined to worship, as evidenced by various forms of devotion in society. Religious individuals express this inclination through their faith in a god, while non-religious individuals may substitute this with self-worship, celebrity adoration, or other forms of idolatry, such as the pursuit of wealth and professional achievements.

Ultimately, I aim to address whether it is possible to avoid this inherent tendency to worship by adhering to what many consider the most logical and reasonable domains of our society: science and politics. Are they really logical? Are they really about evidence and logic with no relation to god or any supernatural nonsense?

NASA’s logo is often noted for resembling a serpent’s tongue. The Apollo mission, named after the Greek god of the sun, included a ritual where astronauts performed communion on the moon, breaking bread and drinking wine. Modern society, often reluctant to honor any deity, is subtly influenced through celebrities, brands, movies, and stars. For instance, the brand Balenciaga can be interpreted as "Baal enci aga," meaning "Baal is the king" in Latin. Similarly, Nickelodeon sounds like "Nic kelo deo," which can be translated as "I don't care about God."
We live in a heliocentric solar system, with "Helios" being a sun god, whose symbol is a globe. The earth's orbital speed is approximately 66,616 mph, though it is often rounded to 67,000 mph. The U.S. Capitol features one of the largest phallic statues, representing the missing penis of the god Osiris. The statue stands 6,665 inches tall and 660 inches wide, with its base measuring 55.5 feet, or 666.000 inches. Atop the Capitol stands his consort, known variously as Isis, Artemis, Columbia, or Liberty. The Capitol itself is adorned with imagery representing this pantheon of Greek gods and goddesses, a topic explored further under my "Presidents" section.
I believe that Holy Scripture can be likened to the red pill and blue pill concept from The Matrix movie, offering a profound choice in how we understand reality. When I approach Scripture with an open mind, setting aside any preconceived moral judgments, I find that it speaks for itself, revealing a clear and comprehensive lens through which to view the world. What’s fascinating is that even for those who may not believe in its divine origin, engaging with Scripture as if it were a narrative—a work of fiction, as some might consider—can lead to surprising insights. By playing along and applying its teachings to our world, I’ve found that it often provides strong evidence not only for the existence of God but also for the reality of other spiritual beings that Scripture warns us about.
